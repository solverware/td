% Introducing the project and its objectives
The development of efficient and cost-effective fuel cell systems is critical for reducing carbon emissions in heavy-duty applications, such as construction and transportation machinery. Project FC352, supported by the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) under award DE-EE200620, aims to advance Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC) technology for heavy-duty vehicles with power outputs ranging from \SI{350}{\kilo\watt} to \SI{1000}{\kilo\watt}. Led by Caterpillar Inc., in collaboration with BorgWarner and Ballard Power Systems, the project focuses on optimizing the balance of plant (BOP) components to meet DOE targets for efficiency, cost, reliability, durability, size, and weight. With a total funding of \$2.5 million, the initiative, running from August 2022 to December 2025, is projected to reduce CO$_2$ emissions by 1.5 million tonnes over a decade if deployed across half of Caterpillar's target product line~\cite{wang2024}.

% Highlighting the role of BOP and thermal management
The BOP, encompassing air supply, thermal management, and other supporting systems, significantly impacts PEMFC performance, contributing 15--25\% of system costs and approximately 25\% of downtime~\cite{wang2024}. Efficient thermal management is essential to maintain PEMFC stack temperatures within the optimal range of \SIrange{60}{80}{\degreeCelsius}, ensuring performance and durability. Heat exchangers, such as recuperators, play a vital role in rejecting heat from the coolant to the environment, reducing the thermal load on the cooling system (see Figure~\ref{fig:recuperator})~\cite{wang2024}. A Pugh analysis was employed to downselect optimal BOP architectures, including potential heat exchanger designs, to achieve these objectives~\cite{wang2024}.

% Connecting to heat exchanger testing
This report presents the results of small-scale heat exchanger tests conducted as part of Project FC352's efforts to enhance BOP efficiency. These tests, aligned with the project's recuperator development activities, evaluate the performance of compact heat exchanger designs. A key benefit of these designs is the reduction of heat rejection to the coolant, which minimizes coolant volume and cooling system size, supporting the project's goals of reducing BOP size to \SI{0.168}{\liter\per\kilo\watt} and cost to \$8.8 per kilowatt~\cite{wang2024}. The following sections detail the experimental setup, results, and implications for heavy-duty PEMFC applications.

\section{Caterpillar's Primary Surface Recuperator Technology}

Caterpillar Inc., known primarily for its heavy machinery, developed a primary surface recuperator (PSR) as a compact heat exchanger designed to enhance the efficiency of gas turbine engines by recovering waste heat from exhaust gases to preheat combustion air. This technology is particularly significant for improving fuel efficiency and reducing emissions in applications like vehicular gas turbines and industrial power systems. Unlike traditional heat exchangers with secondary surfaces (e.g., fins or tubes), primary surface recuperators rely on direct heat transfer through thin, corrugated metal sheets, offering high heat transfer efficiency, compactness, and low pressure drop.

Development of the primary surface technology began in the 1960s and continues to this day. With the purchase of Solar Turbines, the PSR was commercialized in the 1980s for Caterpillar's 5650 gas turbine engine and two of Solar's existing turbines, the Centaur and Saturn. Development of a clean sheet design gas turbine resulted in the Mercury 50 recuperated engine providing much higher thermal efficiencies. A number of other efforts took place for other applications, including microturbines. A factory was built in Houston, Texas in the 1990s and relocated to Tijuana, Mexico in the 2000s where it is still in operation.



Caterpillar has a long history in the development of compact heat exchangers from the 1960s. Caterpillar was interested in gas turbines and heat exchangers can be used to increase their thermal efficiency using the regenerative Brayton cycle also known as the recuperated gas turbine cycle. When Caterpillar acquired Solar Turbines, the compact heat exchangers, referred to as recuperators, were used to market recuperated versions of several existing turbines as well as a clean sheet design, the Mercury 50.

Caterpillar's interest in compact heat exchangers extends well beyond gas turbines for many applications, including fuel-cell technologies. A development in the last decade is the opportunity to manufacture prototypes inexpensively by additive manufacturing. Such prototypes can be used in heat exchanger performance tests. These tests can potentially be used to get the empirical data that is used to predict performance and estimate size of heat exchangers.

\section{Data Analysis}
\subsection{Creating an MS Excel Analysis Spreadsheet}
Data from tests are provided as text files having the CSV format, meaning values are provided in rows with columns separated by columns. Such files use the extension CSV. While CSV file are easily imported into Excel, they are effectively dumb spreadsheets as there are no formulas or calculations. As such, any work done by the spreadsheet would entail editing each spreadsheet to add such functionality. This is impractical when there is more than one spreadsheet to analyze as was the case here.

The solution was to create an application (named PerfTest) that reads the CSV file and creates an actual Excel spreadsheet having the XLSX file extention. This design provides an entry point to add Excel built-in functions into the spreadsheet to analyze the data as well as custom functions to provide physical properties.

The application does several pieces of work:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Only migrates data of interest as the CSV file has many fields which have nothing to do with heat exchanger performance
	\item Performs unit conversions where appropriate
	\item Adds columns for statistical analysis, including averages of thermocouple readings and standard deviations
	\item Adds columns for physical properties of the heat transfer fluid using custom Excel add-in functions
	\item Adds a page that shows all temperature readings with a check for outliers
	\item Adds data goodness checks such as energy balance calculations
	
\end{enumerate}

It created an Excel pared-down spreadsheet with only performance data kept-in.
Did unit conversions for certain fields.

\subsection{Outlier Detection}
An outlier is defined as a data point that significantly deviates from the rest of the observations in a dataset, often identified as being unusually distant from the mean or median, or not fitting the expected pattern or distribution of the data. Statistically, it can be defined as a value that lies beyond a specified threshold, such as more than 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR) \footnote{Tukey, J.W., "Exploratory Data Analysis", Addison-Wesley (1977)} from the first or third quartile, or more than two standard deviations from the mean in a normal distribution.

The Z-score directly measures how many standard deviations a data point is from the mean of a dataset. This relationship is fundamental to its use in statistics, including outlier detection of thermocouple data. 

The Z-score for a data point is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
Z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the mean, $\sigma$ is the standard deviation and  $x - \mu$ is the deviation of the data point from the mean.

In order to be able to quickly determine if outliers exist, the Excel spreadsheet uses \textit{conditional formatting}. If the Z-value exceeds the specified threshold, the cell will display in red. This allows the analyst to see immediately if any points are outliers without having to inspect every data point.

The Engineering Statistics Handbook has a section on outlier detection\citep*{outlierDetection}.

\section{Energy Balance Calculations}
In an ideal heat exchanger (no losses), the heat lost by the hot fluid equals the heat gained by the cold fluid: $Q_h=Q_c$. 

%Technically, the most accurate approach is to use the enthalpy difference:
\begin{equation}
	Q_c=m_c(h_{c,out}-h_{c,in})
\end{equation}
where ( h ) is the specific enthalpy, which accounts for the heat capacities temperature dependence. For constant $C_p$, this simplifies to $m_cC_p\Delta{T}$.
\begin{equation}
h_{c,out}-h_{c,in}\int_{T_{c,out}}^{T_{c,in}}C_p(T)dT
\end{equation}

If the $C_p$ data is discrete data points, one could numerically integrate the data over the interval using any of the numerical integration methods such as Trapezoidal Rule or Simpson's rule. However, in most applications, especially over a narrow temperature range, there simply isn't much variation so this is overkill. Averaging $C_p$ at the inlet and outlet is essentially a shortcut to approximate this integral and is often all that is needed.


There are no strict universal standard for reporting energy balance deviations in heat exchangers, but there are common practices in engineering, especially in testing, research, and industry, that make the results clear and useful. There are four common methods to report energy balance deviation:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Percentage Difference (relative deviation)
	\item Absolute Difference
	\item Ratio of Heat Transfers
	\item Energy Balance Closure
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Percent Difference (relative deviation)}

The percent relative deviation for the heat exchanger energy balance is widely used because it's intuitive, accounts for scale and aligns with engineering tolerances. For example, ASME standards for heat exchanger testing (e.g., ASME PTC 12.5 \footnote{American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME), "Single Phase Heat Exchangers", ASME PTC 12.5-2000 (R2015)}) often suggest reporting imbalances as a percentage of mean heat transfer.

It is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
	{Deviation (\%)} = \frac{\left| |Q_h| - Q_c \right|}{\frac{|Q_h| + Q_c}{2}} \times 100
\end{equation}

\subsection{Absolute Difference}

The energy balance is sometimes reported as absolute difference which shows the raw imbalance and can be useful for troubleshooting specific losses (e.g., heat to the surroundings). 

It is simply the difference in the rate of heat transfer.

\begin{equation}
	{\Delta{Q}=|Q_h| - Q_c}
\end{equation}


\subsection{Ratio of Heat Transfers}

The energy balance may be reported as a ratio of heat transfers, where a value of unity indicates good balance. When it deviates from 1 it highlights the direction of the imbalance. For example, a value greater than 1 indicates external heat input. 

It's typically used as a quick check when presenting to non-technical audiences.

\begin{equation}
	Ratio = \frac{Q_c}{|Q_h|}
\end{equation}


\subsection{Energy Balance Closure}
This is often expressed as a percentage of how "closed" a balance is using the term "closure."

\begin{equation}
	Closure = \frac{\text{Smaller of } |Q_h| \text{ or } Q_c}{\text{Larger of } |Q_h| \text{ or } Q_c} \times 100
\end{equation}

This formula emphasizes how much of the heat transfer is captured, common in academic papers.


We use the percentage difference method here (Method 1) as it is most Common. It's widely used because it’s intuitive, accounts for scale, and aligns with engineering tolerances. For example: ASME standards for heat exchanger testing (e.g., ASME PTC 12.5) often suggest reporting imbalances as a percentage of the mean heat transfer. Lab reports and industrial test summaries frequently cite this metric alongside raw $Q_h$ and $Q_c$.

\section{Colburn Modulus}

The Colburn Modulus is also referred to as the "J-factor."\citep*{kaysandlondon}
\begin{equation}
	j=St\cdot{Pr^{2/3}}
\end{equation}

	The Colburn modulus, denoted a $j$, is defined using the Stanton number ($St$) and the Prandtl number ($Pr$) in the following dimensionless equation:
\begin{equation}
	j=St\cdot{Pr^{2/3}}
\end{equation}
Where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item	$St=\frac{h}{G\cdot{C_p}} $, with 		
	$h$ being the convective heat transfer coefficient, 
	$G$ the mass flux, and $C_p$ the specific heat at constant pressure
	\item  	$Pr=\frac{C_p\cdot{\mu}} {k}  $, where 
	$\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity, 
	$k$ is the thermal conductivity, and 
	$C_p$ is the specific heat at constant pressure
\end{itemize}

This dimensionless relationship is used extensively in heat transfer analysis to correlate heat transfer coefficients with flow and fluid properties.

In heat exchanger thermal analysis predictions, we the value of the heat transfer coefficient. The $j$ factor provides a way to estimate it. If the geometry of the surface has been experimentally studied, we should have a so-called J-graph which correlates the value of $J$ as a function of Reynolds number. We can calculate the Reynolds number of a state point of interest, evaluate it and then get the value of $j$. We can then calculate the heat transfer coefficient directly.

The following steps are followed:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Find the specific heat $C_p$, density $\rho$ viscosity, $\mu$, and the thermal conductivity $k$ of the fluid at the state point of interest
	\item Calculate Reynolds number, $Re$, from the mass flow, geometry of the surface and the fluid viscosity
	\item Look up the Colburn modulus $j$ given the Reynolds number $Re$
	\item Calculate the Stanton number $St$ from the $j$ factor 
	\item Calculate the heat transfer coefficient $h$ from the Stanton number $St$
\end{enumerate}

The calculations are relatively easily when done on a spreadsheet.
One has the state points of temperature and pressure, each in a column. One then has a series of four columns for the physical properties of the fluid, namely $C_p$, $\mu$, $\rho$, $k$.

Another spreadsheet cell should contain the geometry data.



\section{Friction Factor Determination}
When the pressure loss is measured in a test rig, the measured pressure drop includes all pressure drop components of which the frictional losses is just one. There are entrance and exit effects, flow acceleration as well as core friction. We need to determine these other three losses and subtract them from the total pressure drop to determine the friction factor.

The full pressure drop equation is shown in equation~\eqref{eq:pressureDropEqn}.

\begin{equation}		
	\frac{\Delta P}{P_1}=\frac{G^2}{2g_c} \frac {v_1}{P_1} \left[(K_c+1+\sigma^2) +2\left(\frac{v_2}{v_1}-1\right) + 
	f \frac{A}{A_c}  \frac{v_m}{v_1} - (1-\sigma^2-K_e)\frac{v_2}{v_1}  \right]
	\label{eq:pressureDropEqn}		
\end{equation}

Rearranging to solve for the friction factor, we get the following equation:

\begin{equation}
	f = \frac{A_c}{A}\frac{v_1}{v_m} \left[ \frac{2g_c \Delta P}{v_1 G^2} -(K_c+1+\sigma^2)-2\left(\frac{v_2}{v_1}-1\right)+(1-\sigma^2-K_e)\frac{v_2}{v_1}\right]
\end{equation}	

We end up with an equation that is a function of two measured values from the test, i.e., the pressure drop and the mass flow rate. The remaining quantities are functions of the geometry of the surface, the specific volume of the fluid and the Reynolds number.

\subsection{Contraction Loss}
A contraction loss is the specific pressure loss caused by the sudden reduction in flow area such as when the fluid enters the narrower passages of the heat exchanger. 

Contraction losses occur when a fluid enters the narrower passages of a heat exchanger from a larger inlet, such as a header or manifold, resulting in a sudden acceleration of flow. This abrupt reduction in cross-sectional area, often referred to as "entrance loss" or "sudden contraction loss," causes a pressure drop as the fluid's kinetic energy increases, accompanied by the formation of turbulent eddies and a vena contracta—a region where the flow streamlines constrict further. These effects lead to energy dissipation due to flow separation and mixing downstream of the contraction. The severity of the loss depends on the area ratio between the inlet and passage, the fluid's velocity, and the geometry of the transition, making it a key factor in heat exchanger performance optimization.

\subsection{Friction Loss}
Frictional losses can be substantial and are due to viscous drag (the force due to the fluid's viscosity as it flows through a passage), wall friction, which is energy dissipation resultng from the fluid rubbing against the passage's inner surfaces, flow resistance due to the surface roughness and passage geometry and boundary layer drag, i.e., the resistance within the thin layer of fluid near the passage walls where velocity gradients may be significant. There is also a reduction in fluid energy as it is converted to heat due to frictional interactions.

Frictional losses manifest as a fluid moves through the passages of a heat exchanger, where the fluid experiences resistance due to viscous interactions with the passage walls. This phenomenon, often described as "wall friction" or "viscous drag," results in a gradual pressure drop along the passage length as the fluid's mechanical energy is converted into heat through shear stresses within the boundary layer. Factors such as surface roughness, passage geometry, and the fluid's viscosity and velocity significantly influence the extent of these losses. In turbulent flow, additional energy dissipation occurs due to chaotic eddies interacting with the walls, amplifying the frictional pressure loss. Minimizing these losses is crucial for enhancing the efficiency of heat exchanger systems.

A single, experimentally-determined dimensionless factor known as the "friction factor" is used to include all of these effects. 

In Compact Heat Exchangers \citep*{kaysandlondon}, the friction factor used is the Fanning friction factor. This is evident from the context of their work, which focuses on the thermohydraulic performance of compact heat exchanger surfaces, where the Fanning friction factor ($f$) is commonly employed to characterize pressure drop due to fluid flow resistance. The Fanning friction factor is defined as the ratio of wall shear stress to the flow’s kinetic energy per unit volume and is typically used in heat exchanger design to correlate pressure drop with Reynolds number and surface geometry.

The Darcy friction factor, which is four times the Fanning friction factor ($f$), is less commonly used in the context of compact heat exchangers and is more typical in pipe flow calculations. Moody diagrams, for example, use the Darcy friction factor.

\subsection{Expansion Loss}
Expansion losses occur when a fluid exits the confined passages of a heat exchanger into a larger cross-sectional area, such as a header or plenum, leading to a sudden deceleration of flow. This abrupt expansion causes a pressure drop as the fluid's kinetic energy is partially dissipated into turbulent eddies and chaotic flow patterns, rather than being recovered as useful pressure energy. The phenomenon, often termed "exit loss" or "sudden expansion loss," is driven by flow separation and recirculation zones formed at the transition, which increase energy dissipation. Factors like the ratio of passage to outlet area and the fluid's velocity influence the magnitude of these losses, making them a critical consideration in heat exchanger design to optimize overall system efficiency.
